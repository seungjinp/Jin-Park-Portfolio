Project brief & my role

Discover Korea is a non-profit corporation established to expand opportunities for international exchange among multinational youth and to raise Korea's status on the international stage by fostering future diplomatic talents. As a lead UX designer and Front-End developer, I was responsible for redesigning a website for Debate Korea, a subsidiary organization of Discover Korea aiming to promote debate education and revitalize the debate community in Korea.

team & timeline

From April 2020 to June 2020, I worked with 2 content creators and 2 product designers to finish the project. Our responsibilities included resolving UX/UI issues assigned to milestones, contributing ideas and solutions beyond existing issues, and introducing the proper web development process to the team.

problem

Debate is a powerful tool of education that allows individuals to openly express their opinions and develop the skills of critical thinking and persuasion as well as to expand academic and career opportunities. However, debate has yet to become a popular culture in Korea, as many people believe that the debate community is exclusive to the upper class students. Given these stereotypes, many feel intimidated to enter the debate community and don‚Äôt look for opportunities even if they want to participate. Although the organization has been established under the mission statement of resolving this issue, the previous website has not been effective in terms of making the users feel comfortable about the debate community. There were no design principles that aligned with the organization's core values, and the information architecture made it difficult to navigate information about the organization.

I audited the previous website to get an understanding of what prospective debaters experience at Debate Korea. According to the site metrics, a high percentage of users were leaving the homepage without interacting with it. To supplement the issues found in the audit, I analyzed the metrics from the non-profit‚Äôs last site report to identify the key UX problems: poor navigation and way-finding of critical information, lack of concise context, and vague value proposition.

Research process & design goals

Together as a design team, we analyzed other competitors and create a competitor analysis chart. Our potential competitors, such as DebateForall or YTN English Debate Championship, shared the same problem: the website was overloaded with confusing terminology and poorly-designed information architecture, which was overwhelming for students trying to enter the community. Our main goal seemed clear - we have to provide clear and transparent context to help students understand how debating works and how they can enter the community without feeling too overwhelmed.

Based on the user research, the designers, researchers, and I created design principles:

  • Cater to main audience: Students from age 14 to 22, interested in debate education
  • Transparent context: Students need to be informed about the proper process of debating
  • Streamlined information architecture: Key infomation about debating and joining the community should be easy to find so students don‚Äôt feel lost and confused about the process
  • Constant support: Students need to understand how Debate Korea will support them
  • Feeling of belonging: Help students feel connected before signing up to any programs

Gathering the research, the design team and I created the site map, create wireframes, and usability test the website prototype. Throughout this process, our team constantly referred back to the mental model diagram. We would ask ourselves, ‚Äúdoes this design support the thought processes and tasks prospective students ask?‚Äù
